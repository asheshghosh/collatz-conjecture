Hailstone numbers · 3n + 1

The Collatz
Conjecture

Take any whole number. Halve it if it is even; triple it and add one if it is odd. Repeat. Every number ever tried has fallen to 1 — and after nearly a century, no one can prove they all must.

The rule

One line, unanswered since 1937

T(n) = {
n / 2if n is even
3n + 1if n is odd

Lothar Collatz posed it in 1937: apply T over and over, starting anywhere, and you will always reach 1. The orbit of 7 runs 7 22 11 34 17 52 26 13 40 20 10 5 16 8 4 2 1 — sixteen steps, done. These paths rise and plunge so erratically that mathematicians call them hailstone numbers, after the way hail rides updrafts before it finally falls.

The claim is either true for all infinitely many starting values, or false for at least one. Nobody knows which. Proving it has resisted every serious tool in number theory.

Thirteen hundred journeys, grown like coral

Each glowing filament overhead is one number’s complete journey to 1, drawn in reverse — from the root outward. At every step the line bends one way for an even number and the other way for an odd one, an idea from mathematician Edmund Harriss. Nothing organic was simulated: over a thousand orbits, superimposed, produce those fronds on their own. The white-hot spine is where every path crowds through the same final descent; the violet tips are each number setting out alone.

That a bare arithmetic rule yields something this ordered — every branch curling the same way, none escaping — is the conjecture made visible. Try the bend slider or regrow it; the anatomy survives any angle.

Where it stands

Verified to 271. Proven for none of it.

Computers have checked every starting value below 2⁷¹ — that is, below 2.36 × 10²¹, trillions of billions of orbits, all of them falling to 1. In 2019, Terence Tao proved the strongest partial result to date: almost all orbits eventually dip nearly as low as their starting point. Almost all. The gap between almost all and all is where the entire problem still lives.

“Mathematics may not be ready for such problems.” Paul Erdős

A counterexample would have to either grow forever or close a loop that never touches 1 — and any such loop is known to require billions of terms. Until someone finds one, or proves none exists, the coral keeps growing and every branch keeps coming home.
